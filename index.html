Why I built this

A simulation of 25,600 chaotic agents that somehow manage to think together.

The question

I kept returning to a question from Michael Levin's work on collective intelligence in biology: how does a collection of locally-simple agents produce globally-coherent behaviour? Not by design. Not by instruction. Just by being coupled together.

Levin frames this using the idea of platonic space — a structured space of mathematical patterns that assert themselves in physical systems regardless of substrate. His paradigm example is the logistic map:

$$x_{t+1} = r \cdot x_t \cdot (1 - x_t)$$

The Feigenbaum constant ($\delta \approx 4.6692\ldots$) describes the limiting ratio between successive bifurcation intervals in this map. It appears in every smooth single-humped map — regardless of physical details, evolutionary history, or substrate. That's what Levin means by platonic causation: a mathematical structure that asserts itself on whatever system will instantiate it.

This simulation runs that same equation on 25,600 cells simultaneously. I built it to make the transition from local chaos to collective order visible and interactive — something you can feel rather than just read about.

How it works

The grid is 160 × 160 cells, each holding a value $x \in [0,\,1]$. At every timestep, the simulation runs two passes:

Pass 1 — logistic step. Every cell computes its own post-logistic value:

$$f(x) = r \cdot x \cdot (1 - x)$$

Pass 2 — coupling step. Each cell blends its result with its neighbours' results:

$$x_{\text{next}} \;=\; (1-\varepsilon)\,f(x) \;+\; \varepsilon\cdot\overline{f(\text{neighbours})}$$

The order matters. Neighbours are aggregated on post-logistic values $f(x)$, not on raw $x$. This two-pass structure is what allows the coupling to act on comparable quantities and is what produces the rich spatial patterns — changing the order would give a fundamentally different (and less interesting) system.

Cell values are coloured by the Inferno colourmap: $x \approx 0$ is deep purple, $x \approx 1$ is bright yellow. Synchrony tracks how similar all cells are globally (high = one colour dominates); Neighbour Δ tracks the mean absolute difference between adjacent cells (high = sharp domain boundaries visible).
